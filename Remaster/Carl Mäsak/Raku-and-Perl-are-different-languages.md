# Raku and Perl are different languages
    
*Originally published on [5 August 2010](http://strangelyconsistent.org/blog/perl-6-and-perl-5-are-different-languages) by Carl Mäsak.*

Today at the YAPC::EU keynote, the inimitable Larry Wall, accompanied by his guardian angel and his guardian devil, made a poll asking which ones in the audience believed Perl and Raku are the same language, and which ones believed they are two different languages.

I was in the front row, so I didn't see the sea of hands and which way the poll tipped. But on my row, both Patrick Michaud and Jonathan Worthington voted "different". I was slightly surprised to find myself voting "different" as well. I'm the one who only last year wrote [this entry](The-raku-is-not-perl-meme.html) which seems to insist on a "same" vote, if only by criticizing those who take the opposite view.

I think that what's happened in the past year is that there's a bit more room in the "Perl" space. We're now talking of [two different languages in the Perl family](https://www.shadowcat.co.uk/blog/matt-s-trout/-5-v-6.html), and the Perl community being [shaped like a tuning-fork](The-wish-the-tuning-fork-and-the-one-true-heir.html) with its Perl-5 people, Raku people, and Perl-omnivore people. It simply feels safer now to state "different", in a way it didn't last year.

One thing about the tuning fork that I really like is that it's basically two universes in one. We could've had

- (a) Raku taking off early and essentially killing Perl, precluding its renaissance as Modern Perl, or
- (b) Raku floundering so badly and for so long that Perl took over with such force that no-one bothered to develop Raku anymore, and a "Forever Five" condition in the community was announced.

Instead we find ourselves in a junctional universe where both languages are thriving and evolving. And they're different, not least because Perl is older, more mature, and more used in business-oriented environments. But Raku is getting there too, and the two languages will start playing together on increasingly equal footing, just like half-a-generation-apart siblings would.

I like that.
